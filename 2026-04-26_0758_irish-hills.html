Nine Point
RISK
The classic threesome game — 9 points per hole, everyone gets something.

Setup. Use handicaps "off the low man" — the best player plays scratch, the other two get the difference.

Scoring. 9 points per hole, distributed by score:

  5-3-1 · low / middle / high
  4-4-1 · tied for low
  5-2-2 · tied for high
  3-3-3 · three-way tie

Best for: Everyone stays engaged because even the worst score earns a point. The math always adds to 9 — easy to track.

Wolf
RISK
Rotating "wolf" picks a partner after tee shots — or goes solo for double points.

Rotation. The wolf changes every hole. Wolf tees off last so they can see the others' shots.

The choice. After each non-wolf tee shot, the wolf must decide: partner with that player for the hole, or pass. Once you pass, that player is off the table. If the wolf passes on everyone, they must go solo.

Scoring. Wolf + partner vs. third player — low net score wins. Wolf alone vs. the other two — the wolf's score vs. their lowest. Going alone and winning doubles the points.

Best for: Groups that love strategy. The decision of when to go lone wolf is the whole game.

Bingo Bango Bongo
RISK
Three points per hole — first on, closest on, first in.

Three points per hole. Bingo — first ball on the green. Bango — closest to the pin once everyone is on. Bongo — first ball in the hole.

Key rule. Honor goes to whoever is furthest from the hole, not whoever scored lowest on the previous hole. This lets slower players still compete for Bingo.

Scoring. Tally points at the end. Most points wins.

Best for: Mixed skill levels. A shorter hitter who chips well can take Bingo and Bango all day long — perfect equalizer.

Split Sixes
RISK
Like Nine Point but sharper — 6 points per hole, zero for the worst score.

Scoring. 6 points per hole:

  4-2-0 · low / middle / high
  3-3-0 · tied for low
  4-1-1 · tied for high
  2-2-2 · three-way tie

Vs. Nine Point. The worst score earns nothing — a bad hole hurts more. Also called Hollywood in some areas.

Best for: Stronger players who want more punishment on blow-up holes. Skip it if skill gaps are big.

Skins
RISK
Every hole is its own pile of points. Win it outright or it rolls over.

Setup. Each hole is worth 1 skin (1 point).

Scoring. Lowest score on the hole wins the skin. Must win outright — any tie carries over, adding to the next hole.

3-player quirk. Two-way ties are less common than in a foursome, so skins actually get decided more often. Rewards dramatic holes without mountains of carryover.

Best for: Groups that want zero bookkeeping. Just play, lowest score on the hole takes the points.

Rabbit
RISK
Catch the rabbit by winning a hole outright — lose it the same way.

Setup. The rabbit is "loose" at the start of every 6-hole segment (front 9, back 9, or 3 × 6).

Capturing. First player to win a hole outright captures the rabbit. They hold it until someone else wins a hole outright — then the rabbit changes hands.

The end. Whoever holds the rabbit at the end of the segment wins that segment. If the rabbit is still loose (no outright winners), the segment rolls forward to the next.

Best for: Late-round tension. The rabbit can flip on hole 18 and completely change who wins.

Snake
RISK
A putting side-game — whoever 3-putts last loses points to everyone.

Setup. Each 3-putt is worth 1 point. Play as a side-game alongside your main game.

Scoring. Every 3-putt passes the "snake" to that player. Whoever holds it at the end of 18 loses 1 point per 3-putt recorded to each opponent.

Example. Five 3-putts this round. You 3-putted last on 17. You lose 5 points to each playing partner.

Best for: Punishing loose putting. Makes 4-footers feel a lot bigger.
